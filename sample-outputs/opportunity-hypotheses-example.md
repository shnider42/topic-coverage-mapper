# Opportunity Hypotheses

These are starting hypotheses to test through research.

## 1. The audience does not search for “career coaching”

Young adults may search symptoms instead:

- I don't know what career I want
- what should I do after college
- first job advice
- career anxiety
- how to find a mentor

Implication: the content strategy should be built around audience language, not industry language.

## 2. Tactical job search content is crowded

Resume, LinkedIn, and interview content likely has strong supply.

Implication: these topics can support the offer, but should not be the main differentiation.

## 3. Workplace navigation is underdeveloped

Young adults need practical scripts and expectations for professional environments.

Implication: “how work works” may be a stronger wedge than generic career advice.

## 4. Emotional uncertainty is a major entry point

Career confusion and feeling behind may be powerful search surfaces.

Implication: content should be calm, plain-English, and emotionally honest.

## 5. Mentorship content may be too generic

Many videos may say “find a mentor” without showing exactly what to say, whom to ask, or how to maintain the relationship.

Implication: concrete mentorship scripts and examples could stand out.

## 6. The best offer may combine story, direction, and workplace skills

A young adult may not need only a resume review. They may need to understand how to present themselves, choose a target, and operate inside a workplace.

Implication: service packaging should avoid being reduced to commodity resume help.
