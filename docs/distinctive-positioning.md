# Distinctive Positioning

## Goal

Turn the coverage map into a clear client positioning strategy.

The research should answer:

> Where can this client be meaningfully different without pretending the market is empty?

## Common mistake

A weak strategy says:

> There are already career advice videos, so the space is crowded.

A better strategy says:

> Resume tips and interview advice are crowded, but workplace navigation, career confusion, and real mentorship for overwhelmed young adults appear less coherently served.

## Positioning dimensions

### 1. Audience wedge

Be more specific about who this is for.

Examples:

- young adults who feel behind
- first-generation professionals
- recent grads without a network
- early-career people in their first corporate job
- students who do not know what job title to search for
- people with scattered experience who need a career story

### 2. Problem wedge

Focus on a problem that generic advice misses.

Examples:

- “I do not know what career I want”
- “I do not know how offices actually work”
- “I have no network”
- “I have experience, but I cannot explain it”
- “I keep getting generic advice”
- “I need mentorship but do not know how to ask”

### 3. Tone wedge

The tone can be part of the differentiation.

Examples:

- plain-English
- non-hustle
- non-corporate-jargon
- realistic
- calm
- mentor-like
- practical
- emotionally honest

### 4. Format wedge

The format can be the product.

Examples:

- real resume teardown
- “what I would do in your situation”
- career path decision tree
- first 90 days in a job
- script library for workplace conversations
- mock manager conversations
- breakdown of vague job descriptions
- “hidden rules of work” series

### 5. Experience wedge

Use lived experience as proof.

Example:

> Guidance from someone who has worked across enterprise QA, storage, SRE-adjacent troubleshooting, cross-functional initiatives, customer-facing work, Agile coaching, and technical soft-skills training.

This is different from generic influencer advice.

## Candidate positioning statements

### Option A: Practical mentor

> Plain-English career mentoring for young adults who need practical help choosing a direction, telling their story, and navigating work without corporate jargon or hustle-culture noise.

### Option B: Workplace translator

> Career guidance that translates how workplaces actually work for young adults who are entering professional environments without a roadmap.

### Option C: From scattered to coherent

> Helping early-career people turn scattered experience, uncertainty, and half-formed interests into a credible career story and next step.

### Option D: Beyond resume tips

> A career mentoring approach that goes beyond resumes and interviews to teach young adults how to think, communicate, ask for help, and make decisions at work.

## Offer ideas

### Discovery session

A 60–90 minute “career story and next-step” session.

### Career story audit

Review resume, LinkedIn, job targets, and personal narrative.

### First-job survival coaching

A short coaching package for young adults entering their first professional job.

### Mentorship map

Help clients identify who to ask for help, what to ask, and how to follow up.

### Workplace scripts library

Templates for asking questions, requesting feedback, handling mistakes, and navigating ambiguity.

## What to avoid

- Competing directly on generic resume tips
- Overclaiming exact platform coverage
- Saying “no one is doing this”
- Making the content sound like every other career coach
- Hiding the lived-experience advantage behind generic branding
